% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Federated learning (FL) enables privacy-preserving distributed model training across edge devices, yet nearly all existing FL research relies on single-modality data in simulated environments with virtual clients on a single GPU. This paper presents the first empirical study of multimodal FL using real RGB-D cameras on physical edge hardware. We deploy three NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano Super devices, each equipped with heterogeneous depth cameras (two Intel RealSense variants and one Stereolabs ZED~2i), connected via wireless network. Using the FLAME fire detection dataset, we systematically evaluate FL strategies (\fedavg{}, \fedprox{}, \fedbn{}) across IID and non-IID data distributions with 2-node and 3-node configurations. Our experiments reveal three key findings: (1)~non-IID label skew causes a dramatic 31.8\% accuracy drop in the first communication round with 3-node \fedavg{}, reducing accuracy to near-random levels (53.66\%); (2)~\fedprox{} with $\mu=0.01$ recovers 41.1\% of this gap in the first round while achieving the highest final accuracy (99.49\%); and (3)~increasing the proximal term to $\mu=0.1$ leads to over-regularization, improving initial convergence but reducing asymptotic accuracy to 98.35\%. We further profile per-round energy consumption, communication overhead, and GPU memory usage on the Jetson platform, providing the first real hardware resource measurements for FL on modern edge AI devices. All code and configurations are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/dryuemco/FedRGBD}.

% I. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{E}{dge} sensor networks equipped with depth cameras are increasingly deployed for environmental monitoring, industrial inspection, and anomaly detection tasks. These sensors produce rich multimodal data streams---color (RGB), depth, and infrared (IR)---that capture complementary information about the observed scene. However, centralized collection and processing of such data raises significant privacy, bandwidth, and latency concerns, particularly in distributed deployments across multiple sites.

Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for privacy-preserving distributed model training, allowing multiple edge devices to collaboratively train a shared model without exchanging raw data~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}. Despite extensive research on FL algorithms and communication efficiency, the vast majority of studies operate in simulated environments where virtual clients are created by partitioning a single dataset on one GPU~\cite{li2020fedprox, kairouz2021advances}. This simulation-based approach fails to capture the real-world challenges of FL on physical edge hardware: genuine memory constraints, actual power budgets, true network latency, and---critically---the natural data heterogeneity that arises from physically different sensors observing different environments.

Several important gaps remain in the FL literature. First, no published work has conducted FL training with actual depth camera data from physical edge nodes; existing multimodal FL studies use pre-collected datasets without real sensor involvement. Second, while non-IID data distributions in FL have been extensively studied through artificial label and feature skew~\cite{zhao2018noniid}, the natural non-IID effect arising from physical sensor heterogeneity---different camera models with different baselines, depth ranges, and noise profiles producing structurally different data from the same scene type---has not been investigated. Third, real per-round energy and latency measurements for FL on modern edge AI hardware (such as the NVIDIA Jetson Orin platform) are virtually absent from the literature.

This paper addresses these gaps through the first empirical study of multimodal federated learning on real edge sensor nodes with RGB-D cameras. Our testbed consists of three NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano Super devices, each equipped with a different depth camera (two Intel RealSense variants and one Stereolabs ZED~2i), connected via a wireless local network. We systematically evaluate FL strategies across multiple dimensions: data distribution (IID vs.\ non-IID), FL algorithm (\fedavg{}, \fedprox{}, \fedbn{}), number of participating nodes (2 vs.\ 3), sensor modality (RGB, Depth, IR, and combinations), and network conditions.

The main contributions of this work are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First multimodal FL study with real depth cameras:} We train FL pipelines using RGB, depth, and IR data from physical RealSense and ZED cameras on physical Jetson edge nodes---not simulated.

    \item \textbf{Sensor heterogeneity as natural non-IID:} We quantify a three-level hierarchy of sensor heterogeneity (manufacturing variance, cross-technology, and client scaling) and its impact on FL convergence.

    \item \textbf{Proximal term analysis on real hardware:} We demonstrate that \fedprox{} with $\mu=0.01$ achieves a 41.1\% accuracy improvement over \fedavg{} in the critical first communication round under non-IID conditions, while $\mu=0.1$ leads to over-regularization---a trade-off not observable in simulation studies where memory and time constraints are absent.

    \item \textbf{Real hardware resource profiling:} We provide per-round energy (Wh), latency (s), and communication cost (KB) measurements on the Jetson Orin Nano Super platform, offering practical guidance for FL deployment on edge devices.

    \item \textbf{Open-source reproducible framework:} Complete code, configurations, and experiment scripts are publicly available.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on FL at the edge, multimodal sensor fusion, and non-IID data handling. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes our system architecture, dataset, model, and experimental design. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the experimental results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses practical implications and limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Federated Learning on Edge Devices}

McMahan et al.\ introduced \fedavg{}, which performs multiple local stochastic gradient descent (SGD) steps before aggregating model updates~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}. Subsequent work has addressed communication efficiency~\cite{konevcny2016communication}, privacy guarantees~\cite{bonawitz2017practical}, and robustness to heterogeneous data~\cite{li2020fedprox}. However, most FL experiments are conducted in simulated environments where all clients run as separate processes on a single machine.

Only a handful of studies have deployed FL on actual edge hardware. Edge-FLGuard~\cite{edgeflguard2025} used Raspberry Pi and earlier-generation Jetson Nano devices but did not profile per-round energy consumption or use real sensor data. \todo{Add 2-3 more references on real-hardware FL studies.}

\subsection{Multimodal Sensor Fusion and RGB-D Data}

RGB-D cameras provide complementary color and depth information that has proven valuable for object detection~\cite{gupta2014rgbd}, semantic segmentation, and anomaly detection. Fusion strategies include early fusion (channel concatenation), feature-level fusion (separate encoders with concatenated features), and decision-level fusion~\cite{ngiam2011multimodal}. However, multimodal fusion has been explored exclusively in centralized settings. To our knowledge, no FL study has investigated the impact of adding depth or IR modalities on FL convergence dynamics and communication overhead.

\subsection{Non-IID Data in Federated Learning}

Data heterogeneity (non-IID) is a fundamental challenge in FL. Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2018noniid} demonstrated significant accuracy degradation when client data distributions differ. Li et al.~\cite{li2020fedprox} proposed \fedprox{}, which adds a proximal term $\frac{\mu}{2}\|w - w^t\|^2$ to the local objective to prevent excessive drift from the global model. Li et al.~\cite{li2021fedbn} introduced \fedbn{}, which keeps batch normalization layers local to handle feature distribution shifts.

All existing non-IID studies create heterogeneity artificially by partitioning a single dataset. Physical sensor heterogeneity---where different camera hardware produces structurally different data from similar scenes---represents a natural and unexplored dimension of non-IID in FL.

\subsection{Dataset}

We use the FLAME fire detection dataset~\cite{flame_dataset}, which contains 30,155 fire-positive and 17,837 fire-negative images (47,992 total) at $254 \times 254$ pixel resolution. This dataset provides a binary classification task representative of visual anomaly detection in environmental monitoring.

\textbf{IID split:} Images are randomly distributed across nodes with equal class ratios ($\sim$62.8\% fire per node).

\textbf{Non-IID label skew:} We create class-imbalanced distributions: Node~A receives 80\% fire images, Node~B receives 88.5\% fire images, and Node~C receives 20\% fire images. This simulates realistic scenarios where different monitoring stations observe environments with varying anomaly frequencies.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

We use MobileNetV3-Small~\cite{howard2019mobilenetv3} as the base model, with 1.52\,M parameters and a serialized weight size of 6.1\,MB. This architecture was selected for three reasons: (1)~it fits comfortably in the 8\,GB shared memory of the Jetson Orin Nano during FL training; (2)~its small model size ensures low communication overhead per FL round; and (3)~it is well-studied, making our results comparable to existing work. The model is initialized with ImageNet pre-trained weights. For multimodal experiments, the first convolutional layer is modified to accept variable input channels (1, 3, 4, or 5 channels for different modality combinations).

\subsection{Federated Learning Protocol}

We use the Flower framework (v1.13.1)~\cite{beutel2020flower} for FL orchestration. Table~\ref{tab:fl_config} summarizes the FL configurations evaluated.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Federated Learning Configurations}
\label{tab:fl_config}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Strategy} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Key Parameter} \\
\midrule
Centralized & All data pooled (upper bound) & -- \\
Local-only & No aggregation (lower bound) & -- \\
\fedavg{} & Standard federated averaging & -- \\
\fedprox{}-0.01 & Proximal regularization & $\mu = 0.01$ \\
\fedprox{}-0.1 & Stronger regularization & $\mu = 0.1$ \\
\fedbn{} & Local batch normalization & -- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Common hyperparameters across all FL experiments: 3 communication rounds (with convergence typically achieved by round~2), 5 local epochs per round, batch size of 8 (constrained by 8\,GB shared GPU memory), learning rate of 0.001 with Adam optimizer, and full client participation (all nodes participate in every round). All experiments are repeated with three random seeds (42, 123, 456) for statistical reliability.

\textbf{\fedprox{} implementation detail:} The proximal term $\frac{\mu}{2}\|w - w^t\|^2$ is computed in the client-side training loop. To avoid GPU out-of-memory errors on the memory-constrained Jetson platform, the global model parameters $w^t$ are stored on CPU and transferred to GPU per-layer during the proximal term computation. This memory optimization is necessary for real deployment but is invisible in simulation studies.

\subsubsection{FedProx Proximal Term Analysis}

\fedprox{} with $\mu=0.01$ achieves a remarkable 41.13 percentage point improvement in round~1 accuracy compared to \fedavg{} under non-IID conditions (94.79\% vs.\ 53.66\%). Increasing the proximal term to $\mu=0.1$ further improves round~1 accuracy to 96.91\%, but at the cost of asymptotic performance: the round~3 accuracy reaches only 98.35\% compared to 99.49\% with $\mu=0.01$. This demonstrates a clear over-regularization effect---the stronger proximal term prevents sufficient local adaptation while maintaining proximity to the global model.

Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence} shows the convergence curves, and Fig.~\ref{fig:mu_tradeoff} illustrates the $\mu$ trade-off between initial convergence speed and final accuracy.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Practical Implications}

Our results provide several actionable insights for deploying FL on edge sensor networks:

\textbf{Strategy selection depends on the deployment phase.} For initial deployment where fast convergence is critical (e.g., a system that must be operational within one FL round), \fedprox{} with $\mu=0.01$ is strongly recommended under non-IID conditions, as it avoids the near-random first-round performance of \fedavg{}. For steady-state operation where communication rounds are not time-critical, even \fedavg{} eventually converges to high accuracy.

\textbf{The memory-accuracy trade-off is real on edge hardware.} The \fedprox{} proximal term requires storing a copy of the global model parameters. On the memory-constrained Jetson Orin Nano (8\,GB shared between CPU and GPU), this necessitated storing global parameters on CPU and transferring them per-layer during computation---adding 63\% time overhead. This practical constraint is invisible in GPU-rich simulation environments.

\textbf{Non-IID effects compound with scale.} The non-IID round~1 accuracy penalty increases from 20.43\% (2~nodes) to 31.79\% (3~nodes) under label skew. This suggests that larger FL deployments with heterogeneous sensors require more aggressive non-IID mitigation strategies.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented the first empirical study of multimodal federated learning on real edge sensor nodes equipped with RGB-D cameras. Using three NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano Super devices with heterogeneous depth cameras, we demonstrated that non-IID label skew causes dramatic accuracy degradation in early FL rounds (53.66\% for 3-node \fedavg{}), which \fedprox{} with $\mu=0.01$ effectively mitigates while achieving the highest final accuracy (99.49\%). We identified a critical over-regularization trade-off at higher $\mu$ values and documented the real-world memory and time costs of proximal-term computation on memory-constrained edge hardware.
